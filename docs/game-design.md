# Golem Factory: The Clockwork Metropolis

## Game Overview

- **Theme:** A steampunk-inspired world where players take on the role of rival Artificers building clockwork golems and automated factories.
- **Primary Objective:** Amass wealth by generating revenue through goods, crowd-sourcing income via patents, and selling automated machines.

## Core Gameplay Mechanics

### The Time Track (The Grand Mainspring)

- The game board features a spiral time track acting as the central clock tower of the metropolis.
- Turn order is determined by whoever is furthest back on the time track.
- Players spend "Time" as a currency to take massive actions, pushing their token forward and allowing opponents behind them to take multiple smaller turns.
- The track ends at a central Clock Tower, triggering the end of the game when all players reach it.

### Dynamic Automation Triggers

- Players place physical Brass Cog tokens on the time track to act as custom milestone triggers.
- Whenever any player's token lands on or jumps over a Brass Cog, the specific golem associated with that cog activates and runs its programmed workflow.

### Asymmetric Actions (Artificer vs. Golem)

- **Golems:** Handle rigid, automated physical logistics like hauling scrap, refining ore, and loading shipments. They execute steps in a strict, linear order and cannot pivot if resources are unavailable.
- **Artificers:** Handle flexible, intellectual tasks such as drafting components, filing patents, swapping golem parts, and fulfilling contracts.

## Physical Components and Layout

### The Main Board Zones

- **The Assembly Line:** A conveyor belt drafting system along the top edge of the board. Cards slide down a track over time, becoming cheaper to draft the longer they sit.
- **The Loading Docks:** Resource markets on the edge of the board where raw materials (Scrap, Brass, Aether) arrive in full truckload shipments.

### The Artificer's Workshop (Player Tableau)

- Players manage a personal tableau with designated "Assembly Bays" that strictly limit how many golems they can have active at once.
- Players manually upgrade their bays by spending raw materials to buy larger cardboard chassis frames, unlocking the ability to build longer logic loops.

## Low-Code Golem Programming

Building a golem involves snapping physical punch card tiles into a recessed cardboard chassis to create a workflow.

| Component Type | Function | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Logic Core | The Trigger | Dictates when the golem activates (tied to the Brass Cog). |
| Appendages | The Action | The physical labor or logistic steps the golem executes. |
| Chassis | The Constraint | The cardboard frame that dictates how many tiles the golem can hold. |

## Economy and Player Interaction

### The Public Patent Registry

- Players can draft "Advanced Blueprint" cards and file them in a central Patent Registry on the main board.
- The player places a colored token on the blueprint to claim ownership.
- Other players can use standard punch card tiles associated with that blueprint, but they must pay a royalty to the patent holder every time they build a golem with it.

### Liquidating Assets

- Because golems are the ultimate commodity, an Artificer can take an action to permanently remove a fully functioning golem from their tableau and sell it to the city.
- This grants a massive burst of wealth proportional to the golem's complexity, but intentionally breaks the player's automated logistics chain, forcing them to rebuild.
